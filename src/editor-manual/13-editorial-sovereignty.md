# Editorial sovereignty

This is the chapter most software manuals skip. Telaris's structure (no folders, no review queue, no central vocabulary, no "wrong" tag) only works if editors hold the position with intention. The choices you make are sovereign. They are not validated by anyone else. They are not run through an approval flow. They are not silently corrected by an algorithm. The decision to apply a keyword is yours; the decision to leave one off is also yours.

This chapter is about what that means in practice.

## What sovereignty does and does not include

Editorial sovereignty in Telaris means:

- You decide what goes into a galaxy and what stays out.
- You decide which keywords to apply and which to omit.
- You decide which connections (on the keyword canvas) belong and which do not.
- You decide which wormholes to accentuate and which to leave plain.
- You decide when to retire a wormhole and when to keep it.

It does **not** mean:

- You can edit other editors' galaxies. Each galaxy has an owning editor (or set of editors) assigned by the operator; you can only act inside galaxies you have been assigned to.
- You can override a source community's withdrawal of consent. If material in your galaxy was contributed by a community and they withdraw it, the answer is to remove the material, not to argue.
- You can publish without restraint. Your operator runs the instance and is responsible for what visitors see; if the operator asks you to remove something for a reason that crosses your editorial line, that is a conversation between you and the operator, not a feature in the software.

The line is the line; the software does not police it; the responsibility lives with the editor.

## When *not* to apply a keyword

The instinct of many archive systems is to tag everything. Telaris's design rewards the opposite: a keyword applied carelessly does worse than no keyword. Every keyword you attach is a connection to every other wormhole carrying the same word; if the connection is not meaningful, the visitor's path gets noisier.

Practical refusals:

- Refuse a keyword that names the obvious. *Plant* in a plant galaxy. *Coast* on a coastal-plant wormhole. These carry no signal because every wormhole nearby would share them.
- Refuse a keyword that is wrong in a way nobody else will notice. If a wormhole is *technically* native but the source community describes it as *introduced for centuries*, the editorial truth is more useful than the botanical one. Apply *introduced* (or both); the keyword strip is your editorial sentence.
- Refuse a keyword that puts a wormhole in a category it should not stand for. A wormhole about a community's ceremonial plant should not necessarily be tagged *edible* even when it is also eaten; the editorial frame may be ceremonial first, food incidentally.

The fewer keywords you assign, the more deliberate each connection feels. Five precise tags read better than fifteen comprehensive ones.

## When *not* to draw a connection

The keyword canvas (chapter 8) lets you draw lines between any two keywords. The discipline is to draw only the lines that say something the visitor would not infer from the keywords themselves.

- Refuse a connection between two keywords that obviously co-occur. *Native* and *perennial* both appear on many plants; if the editorial point is just "these often go together," the connection adds noise.
- Refuse a connection whose note you cannot write. If you cannot write a one-sentence note explaining why two keywords belong, the connection is decorative; leave it out.
- Refuse a connection that contradicts your editorial frame. Two keywords that *technically* relate but that the galaxy's framing treats as distinct should stay disconnected; the absence of a line is also an editorial choice.

The canvas reads best when it has fewer, sharper lines than it has chips.

## Withdrawal of consent

Some Telaris content originates with source communities (a community that contributed photographs, a culture that hosts the practice you are documenting, an interview subject whose words you are republishing). The consent of the source community is the editorial floor, not a feature you can turn off.

When a source community withdraws consent:

- Remove the wormhole. Do not argue, do not negotiate, do not delay.
- If the material informed other wormholes (the wormhole was the source of a keyword, of a connection drawn on the canvas), follow the implications: a keyword that no longer maps to anything is a keyword to remove; a connection that no longer connects anything is one to delete.
- Tell your operator. The operator may need to clear the material from snapshots or from federated mirrors; that is their work, not yours, but they need the signal.

The platform does not have an "editorial review" that overrides community consent. Withdrawal is final.

## When *not* to publish a wormhole at all

A wormhole can be drafted, then never published. The simplest way: keep it in a galaxy whose visibility is restricted (your operator can configure this; some instances have "draft" galaxies that visitors do not see). The pattern that reads well:

- Draft galaxies for in-progress work.
- Public galaxies for material you are willing to stand by.
- Move wormholes from draft to public when the editorial decision is firm.

If your instance does not have a draft-galaxy pattern, ask your operator about setting one up; the difference between *drafting* and *publishing* is a real editorial distinction, and the software should support it.

## There is no review queue

Telaris does not have an approval flow. There is no "Submit for review" button, no editor-in-chief, no central pair of eyes that decides what is good enough. The decision to publish lives in the moment you save the wormhole.

This is by design. A review queue would import the assumption that one editor's judgement needs another's approval, which is the platform pattern Telaris is built to refuse. The cost is responsibility: when you save, you publish.

Two practices that hold up:

- **Read the wormhole as a visitor before saving.** The View button in the actions menu opens the wormhole's info card in read-only mode; this is the moment to catch a tag you would not stand by, a description that is sharper than you meant, a keyword that connects somewhere you did not intend.
- **Conversation with other editors, where they exist.** Telaris does not formalise it, but the conversations between editors of related galaxies are how a network keeps its editorial coherence. If your instance has more than one editor, find a rhythm for talking about the choices; the software does not, and will not.

## Things worth knowing

- **There is no "edit history" surface for visitors.** Visitors see what is currently published; they do not see the history of a wormhole's edits. If a change is editorial (a tag added, a description revised, a media file replaced), it lands silently; if you want visitors to know the work has been revised, write it in the description.
- **A wormhole's editor can change.** Your operator can re-assign galaxies; a galaxy you authored last month may be edited by someone else next month. Treat your editorial choices as durable to that succession: leave the galaxy in a state another editor can pick up.
- **Editorial sovereignty does not extend to the operator's domain.** Federation decisions, peer admission, snapshot scheduling, key rotation, and any other operational choice belong to the operator. Editors are not consulted on them; nor are operators consulted on editorial choices. The line is firm.
- **Telaris's voice is observational, not promotional.** The brand book describes the voice in detail; for editors, the practical takeaway is that the platform does not write your descriptions, does not sweeten your phrasing, does not soften your refusal. Your prose is your prose. The platform frames it; the framing does not edit it.
